Janet didn't grow up with the answers. As a teenager and young adult, she struggled with identity — the quiet, persistent kind of confusion that doesn't announce itself but shapes every decision you make. She remembers looking around rooms, wondering if anyone would reach out. Wondering if anyone saw what she was carrying. Wishing someone would walk her through the hard questions she didn't even have words for yet.

Nobody did. So she learned — through faith, through failure, through years of honest reflection — to find her own footing. And once she found it, she couldn't stop wanting that for other young people.

"I looked around the room as a teenager, wondering who would reach out. I wished anyone would walk me through stuff back then. That wish became my purpose."

That's the origin of her burden for young people. Not theory. Not a programme she read about. A lived ache that grew into a calling. Over more than a decade, she has poured that calling into classrooms, campuses, church halls, and community centres — in Kenya and across the continent.

She has stood on the banks of rivers watching lives transformed at baptism. She has sat with young people in the scorching afternoon heat, praying for souls who didn't yet know they were worth praying for. She has designed curricula for corporate institutions and university programmes, lectured at Strathmore and Mount Kenya University, trained caregivers in vulnerable communities, and built youth mentorship programmes from the ground up at Akili Consult.

Her formal training spans a First Class degree in Information Technology from Jomo Kenyatta University of Agriculture and Technology (JKUAT) and a Master's in ICT for Development from the University of Manchester — but the education that shapes Thrive most profoundly was earned in rooms full of young people who needed someone to see them clearly and tell them the truth.

Today, Janet serves as Assistant Director of Marketing, PR and Alumni Affairs at the Adventist University of Africa, serves on the board of Generation Youth Christ Africa, and regularly visits high schools and university campuses as a speaker. She is also a trained medical missionary, a photographer, and someone who believes deeply that every young person deserves the chance to thrive — not just survive.

"She writes with honesty about removing the mask — about the gap between the life we project publicly and who we actually are in private. That's the work Thrive is built to do."

Her blog, which she has kept since 2009, captures her voice most directly. She writes about real humility — not the kind that shrinks, but the kind that depends on God's strength. About fear and faith. About all things working together for good, even when the plan falls apart. About what it means to live from the inside out. These aren't abstract convictions. They are the hard-won conclusions of a life lived with open hands.

Thrive is her invitation to young adults to do in three days what took her years — to name who they are, stand for something real, and step into a direction that is genuinely theirs.

Why she built this

She knows the itch.

Thrive wasn't designed in a conference room. It was built from the inside out — from Janet's own experience of the questions it addresses, and from years of watching young people carry those same unanswered questions into adulthood.

She has travelled to multiple countries as a missionary and trainer, desiring as many young people as possible to have excellent characters — lives that are a living witness of integrity and Christian grace to everyone they meet.

That desire has never been about programmes. It has always been about people. About the teenager sitting in the room, wondering if anyone will speak to what they're actually carrying.
